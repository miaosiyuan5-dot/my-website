A Thousand-Year Tradition

The History of Yixing Zisha

The story of Yixing Zisha pottery begins in the Song Dynasty (960–1279 AD), when craftspeople in the Yixing region of Jiangsu Province first discovered that the mineral-rich clay from Huanglongshan — unlike any clay found elsewhere — could be formed into teapots of extraordinary beauty and functional excellence without glaze.

By the Ming Dynasty (1368–1644), Yixing Zisha teapots had become inseparable from the gongfu tea ceremony. Scholar-officials and poets commissioned personalised pots bearing their seal and calligraphy; the names of master potters like Shi Dabin (时大彬) and Chen Hongshou (陈鸿寿) became as celebrated as the literati they served.

The Qing Dynasty saw Yixing teapots travel beyond China's borders — to Japan, South-East Asia, and eventually Europe, where they became prototypes for the early Delft and Meissen red stoneware tradition. By the twentieth century, Yixing pottery had been recognised as an Intangible Cultural Heritage of China, with the title of "Craft Master" (工艺美术师) bestowed by the state to fewer than five hundred living practitioners.

Today, the tradition continues in the workshops of Yixing — still using the same ore, the same hand techniques, the same bamboo tools — while a new generation of artisans brings fresh artistic vision to an ancient form.

Our Philosophy

What We Believe

A teapot is not a product. It is a conversation between the clay, the artisan, the tea, and the person who drinks it.
壶不是商品，而是泥与人、匠与茶之间永恒的对话。

Uncompromising Authenticity

We source only original mine ore, verify each artisan's credentials and lineage, and reject any teapot that shows signs of mold use, chemical treatment, or blended clay.

Fair Partnership

Our artisans set their own prices. We take a fixed logistics margin and never negotiate down. The craftsperson's livelihood must be as sustainable as the clay tradition they carry.

Cultural Stewardship

Part of every sale funds the Dingyuan Apprenticeship Programme, which places young people from Yixing with senior craft masters for three-year residencies — ensuring the next generation can inherit what centuries built.

The Makers

Our Master Artisans

定渊签约匠人

Every teapot in our collection is traceable to a single artisan. These are three of the masters whose work you will find here.

Chen Wei

陈威 Senior Craft Master · 高级工艺美术师

With 38 years at the wheel — or rather, away from it — Chen Wei is renowned for his mastery of the Classic Round and Antique Round forms. He trained under the late Gu Jingzhou's student, and his personal seal is among the most sought-after in Yixing. His pots are held in collections at the Palace Museum and the Victoria & Albert Museum.

Lin Fang

林芳 Craft Master · Engraving Specialist

Lin Fang began studying clay carving (陶刻) at age fourteen in her grandmother's workshop. Today she is one of fewer than forty female holders of the National Senior Craft Master title. Her signature style combines Song-era landscape motifs with spare, contemporary negative space — a sensibility formed by her parallel practice as a ink-wash painter.

Zhang Bo

张博 Craft Master · Form Innovator

The youngest of our core artisans at 31, Zhang Bo graduated from the Jiangsu Ceramic Institute before spending five years as an apprentice under Chen Wei. His work explores the boundary between classical Zisha forms and contemporary sculpture — particularly his use of Duanni (缎泥) clay, whose pale mineral tones complement his architectural, precision-formed pots.
